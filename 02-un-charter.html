Chapter 02 · International Law

The United Nations Human Rights Charter

Origins, drafting, structure, and legal status of the UDHR — the document that reshaped international law after WWII.

High Yield

The United Nations Human Rights Charter most commonly refers to the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR), adopted by the UN General Assembly on 10 December 1948 in Paris, through Resolution 217A (III). It was drafted in direct response to the atrocities of World War II and the Holocaust, with the founding members vowing that such large-scale violations of human dignity must never be allowed to happen again.

Definition The UDHR is a foundational international document that sets out, in 30 articles, the basic rights and fundamental freedoms to which every human being is entitled, "without distinction of any kind."

Background and Drafting

The UN Commission on Human Rights was tasked with drafting the document. Eleanor Roosevelt, widow of US President Franklin D. Roosevelt, chaired the drafting committee and is one of the most recognised figures associated with the Declaration's creation. The drafting committee deliberately avoided referencing any single culture, religion, or political system in order to make the Declaration's language genuinely universal.

Structure of the Charter

The UDHR is composed of a Preamble and 30 Articles, broadly organised into these categories:

  • Articles 1–2: Foundational principles — equality, dignity, and non-discrimination.
  • Articles 3–21: Civil and political rights — life, liberty, fair trial, freedom of expression, freedom of religion, right to participate in government.
  • Articles 22–27: Economic, social, and cultural rights — right to work, education, social security, and participation in cultural life.
  • Articles 28–30: Implementation clauses — the social order necessary to realise these rights, duties to the community, and limits on the exercise of rights.

Legal Status

The UDHR itself is a declaration, not a binding treaty — it was adopted as "a common standard of achievement for all peoples and all nations" rather than a legally enforceable instrument. However, many of its provisions have since become part of customary international law, and it directly inspired binding treaties, most notably the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR), together forming what is known as the International Bill of Human Rights.

Exam tip A favourite trick question: "Is the UDHR legally binding?" The correct nuanced answer is — not directly as a declaration, but its principles are widely accepted as customary international law, and it gave rise to binding covenants (ICCPR and ICESCR).

Significance

The UDHR is considered a milestone document because it was the first time the international community agreed, in one text, on a comprehensive set of rights belonging to all humans simply by virtue of being human — regardless of nationality, place of residence, sex, or any other status. It directly inspired the development of international human rights law and remains the most translated document in the world.
