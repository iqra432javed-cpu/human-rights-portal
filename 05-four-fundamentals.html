Chapter 05 · Core Concepts

Four Fundamentals in HR: Freedom, Equality, Justice, Dignity

The four philosophical pillars that underpin the entire human rights framework, and how they connect.

High Yield

The course outline names four fundamental concepts underpinning human rights: freedom, equality, justice, and human dignity. These are not separate, unrelated ideas — they function together as the philosophical pillars on which the entire human rights framework rests.

1. Freedom (Liberty)

Definition Freedom refers to a person's ability to think, speak, believe, move, and act according to their own will, without unjust interference from the state or others — within the limits necessary to respect the same freedom of others.

Freedom underlies civil and political rights such as freedom of expression (Article 19, UDHR), freedom of religion (Article 18), and freedom of movement (Article 13). Without freedom, a person cannot meaningfully exercise any other right.

2. Equality

Definition Equality means that all human beings, regardless of race, gender, religion, ethnicity, or social status, are entitled to the same rights and protections, and must be treated alike before the law.

Equality is captured most directly in Articles 1 and 2 of the UDHR. It does not mean that everyone must be identical, but that no one's dignity or entitlement to rights is lesser because of who they are.

3. Justice

Definition Justice refers to fairness in the way laws, institutions, and societies treat individuals — including fair trial, equal access to legal remedy, and proportional consequences for wrongdoing.

Justice operationalises the other fundamentals: it is the mechanism through which freedom and equality are protected and enforced when violated. Articles 7–11 of the UDHR (equality before the law, right to remedy, presumption of innocence) all express the justice principle.

4. Human Dignity

Definition Human dignity is the inherent worth of every person simply because they are human — the foundational value from which all other human rights are derived.

Dignity is mentioned in the very first line of the UDHR's Preamble: "recognition of the inherent dignity... of all members of the human family." It is considered the root concept — freedom, equality, and justice all exist in order to protect and express human dignity.

Human DignityThe root value — why rights exist at allPreamble; Article 1

Exam tip A common 10-mark question is "Discuss the four fundamentals of human rights." Treat human dignity as the foundation, then explain how freedom, equality, and justice each protect a different dimension of that dignity — this gives your answer a clear logical structure rather than four disconnected paragraphs.
