Chapter 09 · Regional Focus

Human Rights in South Asia: Issues

Major recurring human rights challenges across the South Asian region and their underlying root causes.

High Yield

South Asia — home to over a fifth of the world's population — faces distinctive and persistent human rights challenges shaped by poverty, weak enforcement capacity, political instability, and deep social inequality. This chapter surveys the major recurring issues examined under this topic.

Major Human Rights Issues in South Asia

1. Poverty and Economic Rights

Widespread poverty across the region undermines the practical enjoyment of economic and social rights — adequate standard of living, healthcare, and housing — even where these rights are constitutionally guaranteed on paper. Poverty is also a leading driver of other violations, such as child labour and bonded labour.

2. Gender-Based Discrimination

Women across South Asia continue to face unequal access to education, employment, inheritance, and political participation, along with high rates of domestic violence, early/forced marriage, and "honour"-based violence in some communities. Legal reform has often outpaced actual social change, leaving a gap between law and lived reality.

3. Child Labour and Exploitation

Despite ratifying core ILO and UN conventions, several South Asian states continue to report significant child labour, particularly in agriculture, brick kilns, domestic work, and small-scale manufacturing — driven primarily by household poverty (covered in depth in the Rights of Children chapter).

4. Minority and Religious Rights

Religious and ethnic minorities in parts of South Asia face barriers to equal access to education, public employment, and in some cases, basic civil registration — for example, some minority and Ahmadi communities reportedly encounter denial of school enrolment in parts of Pakistan due to religious identity.

5. Access to Education

Out-of-school children remain a critical regional issue — driven by poverty, inadequate infrastructure, language-of-instruction barriers for ethnic minority languages, lack of trained teachers, and gendered social norms that disproportionately keep girls out of school in some areas.

6. Weak Enforcement and Rule of Law

Many South Asian states have ratified major human rights treaties and even passed strong domestic legislation, but enforcement remains inconsistent due to under-resourced labour inspectorates, slow judicial processes, and limited institutional capacity at the provincial/local level.

7. Human Trafficking and Forced Labour

Cross-border trafficking — including of children — for forced labour, domestic servitude, and exploitation remains a documented problem in border regions, often linked to poverty, statelessness, and lack of legal identity documentation.

Why These Issues Persist: Common Root Causes

Root CauseConsequence
Poverty and population pressureFamilies rely on child labour; reduced access to education and healthcare
Weak institutional capacityLaws exist on paper but are poorly enforced (e.g. understaffed labour inspectorates)
Social and cultural normsGender discrimination, early marriage, and minority exclusion persist despite legal protections
Devolved governance challengesResponsibility for social sectors shifted to provinces, leading to uneven implementation across regions
Exam tip When discussing "HR issues in South Asia," always link each issue back to a root cause (poverty, weak enforcement, social norms) — examiners reward analytical depth over a simple list of problems.
