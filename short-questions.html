Q1 What is the Farewell Sermon and when was it delivered? 5 marks ⌄
The Farewell Sermon (Khutbatul Wada) was delivered by the Holy Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) on the 9th of Dhul Hijjah, 10 A.H., at Mount Arafat during his only Hajj. It is regarded as one of history's earliest comprehensive human rights charters, addressing equality, sanctity of life, and women's rights over a thousand years before the UDHR.
Q2 Name three human rights themes found in the Farewell Sermon. 5 marks ⌄
  • Sanctity of life and property — declared sacred and inviolable.
  • Equality of all human beings regardless of race, judged only by piety.
  • Rights of women — mutual rights and kind treatment between spouses.
Q3 How does the Farewell Sermon's equality principle compare to UDHR Article 1? 5 marks ⌄
The sermon states all humans descend from one father and mother, and no Arab has superiority over a non-Arab except through piety. UDHR Article 1 similarly declares all human beings born free and equal in dignity and rights — both reject discrimination based on origin or status.
Q4 What did the Farewell Sermon say about usury (riba)? 5 marks ⌄
The sermon declared all pre-Islamic forms of interest-based dealing (riba) void and abolished. This reflects an early principle of economic justice, connecting to the modern right to a fair standard of living and protection from exploitative financial practices.
Q5 When and where was the UDHR adopted? 5 marks ⌄
The Universal Declaration of Human Rights was adopted by the UN General Assembly on 10 December 1948 in Paris, through Resolution 217A (III), in direct response to the atrocities of World War II and the Holocaust.
Q6 Who chaired the drafting committee of the UDHR? 5 marks ⌄
Eleanor Roosevelt, widow of US President Franklin D. Roosevelt, chaired the UN Commission on Human Rights' drafting committee responsible for producing the UDHR.
Q7 Is the UDHR legally binding? Explain briefly. 5 marks ⌄
The UDHR itself is a declaration, not a binding treaty — it was adopted as a common standard of achievement rather than enforceable law. However, many of its principles have become customary international law, and it directly inspired binding covenants like the ICCPR and ICESCR.
Q8 What is the International Bill of Human Rights? 5 marks ⌄
It refers to three documents together: the UDHR (1948), the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR, 1966), and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR, 1966) — which turned the UDHR's principles into binding treaty obligations.
Q9 How many articles does the UDHR contain? 5 marks ⌄
The UDHR contains a Preamble followed by 30 Articles, covering foundational principles, civil and political rights, economic and social rights, and implementation/limitation clauses.
Q10 Define human rights in one sentence. 5 marks ⌄
Human rights are the basic rights and freedoms that belong to every person from birth until death, simply by virtue of being human, regardless of nationality, sex, ethnicity, religion, or any other status.
Q11 List four characteristics of human rights. 5 marks ⌄
  • Inherent — belong to a person simply for being human.
  • Universal — apply equally to everyone, everywhere.
  • Inalienable — cannot be taken away or given up.
  • Indivisible and interdependent — all categories of rights are equally important.
Q12 Differentiate between human rights and legal rights. 5 marks ⌄
Human rights exist independently of any country's legal system and belong to a person simply for being human. Legal rights, by contrast, are granted specifically by a country's laws and can be altered or withdrawn by that country's legislature.
Q13 Name the five broad categories of human rights. 5 marks ⌄
Civil rights (life, fair trial), political rights (voting, expression), economic rights (work, property), social rights (education, health), and cultural rights (participation in cultural life, language rights).
Q14 Name two pre-1948 milestones in the evolution of human rights. 5 marks ⌄
The Magna Carta (1215), which limited the power of the English king and established that rulers are subject to law; and the French Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen (1789), which articulated liberty, equality, and fraternity during the Enlightenment.
Q15 Why is World War II considered a turning point for human rights? 5 marks ⌄
The systematic genocide of the Holocaust proved that domestic law alone could not protect human dignity, since the state itself could become the principal violator of its citizens' rights. This pushed the world toward an international framework, culminating in the UDHR in 1948.
Q16 What are the three generations of human rights? 5 marks ⌄
First generation: civil and political rights (liberty-oriented, from Enlightenment-era revolutions). Second generation: economic, social, and cultural rights (equality-oriented, from industrialization). Third generation: collective/solidarity rights (right to development, environment, peace).
Q17 What treaty established the rights of the child internationally, and when? 5 marks ⌄
The Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC) was adopted by the UN in 1989. It is the most widely ratified human rights treaty in the world and sets out comprehensive civil, political, economic, social, and cultural rights for children.
Q18 Name the four fundamentals of human rights as per the course outline. 5 marks ⌄
Freedom, equality, justice, and human dignity — these form the philosophical foundation on which the entire human rights framework rests.
Q19 Why is human dignity considered the 'root' fundamental? 5 marks ⌄
Human dignity refers to the inherent worth of every person simply because they are human. It is mentioned in the very first line of the UDHR's Preamble, and freedom, equality, and justice all exist as mechanisms to protect and express this underlying dignity.
Q20 How does justice function differently from freedom and equality? 5 marks ⌄
Freedom and equality describe what a person is entitled to (liberty to act, and equal treatment), while justice is the enforcement mechanism — fair trial, legal remedy, and proportional consequences — that protects freedom and equality when they are violated.
Q21 Which UDHR articles cover the right to a fair trial? 5 marks ⌄
Articles 9, 10, and 11 of the UDHR cover this area: freedom from arbitrary arrest (Art. 9), right to a fair public trial (Art. 10), and the presumption of innocence (Art. 11).
Q22 What does UDHR Article 26 guarantee? 5 marks ⌄
Article 26 guarantees the right to education, including free and compulsory education at the elementary stage, and the principle that education should be directed toward the full development of the human personality.
Q23 Name three economic, social, or cultural rights covered in the UDHR. 5 marks ⌄
The right to social security (Art. 22), the right to work and fair wages (Art. 23), and the right to an adequate standard of living (Art. 25).
Q24 What does Article 30 of the UDHR state? 5 marks ⌄
Article 30 states that nothing in the Declaration may be interpreted as permitting any state, group, or person to engage in any activity aimed at destroying any of the rights and freedoms set forth in it.
Q25 What does 'inalienability' mean in human rights? 5 marks ⌄
Inalienability means human rights are unconditional — they do not have to be bought, inherited, or earned, and they cannot be taken away, renounced, or transferred, regardless of a person's conduct.
Q26 Explain the principle of indivisibility. 5 marks ⌄
Indivisibility means all human rights — civil, political, economic, social, and cultural — carry equal status, with none superior to another. The enjoyment of one right is often connected to the enjoyment of others (interdependence).
Q27 What does the universality of human rights mean? 5 marks ⌄
Universality means human rights apply to every person, everywhere, simply because they are human, regardless of nationality, place of residence, gender, ethnicity, religion, or political belief, as stated in UDHR Article 1.
Q28 How are indivisibility and universality different? 5 marks ⌄
Universality concerns who rights apply to (everyone, everywhere), while indivisibility concerns the relationship between different rights (all rights are equally important; none takes priority over another).
Q29 What is cultural relativism in the context of human rights? 5 marks ⌄
Cultural relativism is the argument that human rights frameworks reflect specifically Western liberal philosophy, and may not adequately fit non-Western cultural, religious, or social contexts, challenging the claim that human rights are truly universal.
Q30 What did the Bangkok Declaration (1993) state? 5 marks ⌄
Asian states affirmed their commitment to human rights as interdependent and indivisible while simultaneously stressing sovereignty, non-interference, and prioritising economic and developmental rights — reflecting discomfort with how universality is sometimes applied.
Q31 Give one argument supporting the universality of human rights. 5 marks ⌄
Concepts resembling human rights appear independently across multiple cultures and eras — for example, the Farewell Sermon's emphasis on equality and the Magna Carta's limits on rulers — suggesting shared underlying values that transcend any single culture.
Q32 What is the 'moral floor, not cultural ceiling' argument? 5 marks ⌄
This argument holds that universality only sets a minimum floor of protection (such as no torture, no slavery) within which diverse cultural practices can still exist, rather than requiring the erasure of all cultural diversity.
Q33 Name three major human rights issues in South Asia. 5 marks ⌄
Poverty undermining economic rights, gender-based discrimination against women, and persistent child labour despite ratified international conventions.
Q34 Why does weak enforcement remain a problem in South Asia despite strong legislation? 5 marks ⌄
Many South Asian states have ratified major treaties and passed domestic laws, but enforcement is inconsistent due to under-resourced labour inspectorates, slow judicial processes, and limited institutional capacity at the provincial or local level.
Q35 What role does devolved governance play in HR enforcement challenges? 5 marks ⌄
After constitutional amendments shifting responsibility for social sectors to provinces (e.g. Pakistan's 18th Amendment), implementation has become uneven across regions, since provincial capacity and political will vary significantly.
Q36 Name one minority rights concern documented in South Asia. 5 marks ⌄
Religious and ethnic minorities in parts of the region face barriers to equal access to education and public employment — for example, some minority communities reportedly encounter denial of school enrolment in parts of Pakistan due to religious identity.
Q37 What does CEDAW stand for and when was it adopted? 5 marks ⌄
CEDAW is the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women, adopted in 1979. It is the most comprehensive treaty dedicated to women's rights, often called an international 'bill of rights for women.'
Q38 What does UDHR Article 16 guarantee regarding women? 5 marks ⌄
Article 16 guarantees equal rights for men and women in marriage, during marriage, and at its dissolution — establishing equality within the family unit as a human rights matter.
Q39 Name two key areas of concern for women's rights in South Asia. 5 marks ⌄
Education (persistent gender gaps in literacy and enrolment) and political participation (underrepresentation in senior decision-making roles despite quota systems).
Q40 How does the Farewell Sermon connect to women's rights? 5 marks ⌄
The sermon instructed believers to treat women with kindness and recognised mutual rights between spouses, supporting the argument that respect for women's rights is consistent with, not foreign to, local religious tradition.
Q41 Which articles of Pakistan's Constitution protect women against discrimination? 5 marks ⌄
Article 25 guarantees equality of citizens, Article 26 prohibits sex-based discrimination in access to public places, and Article 27 prohibits discrimination in employment in the service of Pakistan.
Q42 What is the CRC and when was it adopted? 5 marks ⌄
The Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC) was adopted in 1989. It is the most widely ratified human rights treaty in the world, setting out comprehensive rights for individuals under 18.
Q43 Define child labour as per ILO standards. 5 marks ⌄
Child labour is work that deprives children of their childhood, potential, and dignity, and is harmful to their physical and mental development — typically involving children below the minimum legal working age, or hazardous work regardless of age.
Q44 Name the two key ILO conventions on child labour. 5 marks ⌄
The Minimum Age Convention (No. 138, 1973), setting the general minimum employment age at 15 (or 14 in some developing countries), and the Worst Forms of Child Labour Convention (No. 182, 1999), banning slavery, trafficking, and hazardous work.
Q45 Name three root causes of child labour. 5 marks ⌄
  • Household poverty — families depend on children's earnings for survival.
  • Lack of access to education — underfunded schools and distance push children toward work.
  • Weak law enforcement — under-resourced labour inspectorates fail to prevent violations.
Q46 What is the 'worst forms first' approach to eliminating child labour? 5 marks ⌄
It is the internationally accepted strategy of prioritising the elimination of the most hazardous and exploitative forms of child labour immediately, while pursuing the broader elimination of all child labour through poverty reduction and expanded education access.
Q47 Name one Pakistani law addressing child labour. 5 marks ⌄
The Employment of Children Act, 1991, which makes it illegal for children under 14 to work in specified occupations.
Q48 What constitutional article in Pakistan prohibits child labour? 5 marks ⌄
Article 11 of Pakistan's Constitution prohibits all forms of forced labour, including child labour.
Q49 Why was the Vienna Declaration of 1993 significant for the indivisibility principle? 5 marks ⌄
It formally reaffirmed that civil/political rights and economic/social/cultural rights are equally important and indivisible, resolving the Cold War-era split where Western and Soviet-bloc states had emphasised different categories of rights.
Q50 What is the difference between human rights and fundamental rights? 5 marks ⌄
Fundamental rights are the domestic legal expression of human rights within a particular country's constitution (e.g. Pakistan's Articles 8–28), while human rights exist as a broader international concept independent of any single country's legal framework.
