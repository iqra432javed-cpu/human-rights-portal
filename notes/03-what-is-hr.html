Chapter 03 · Core Concepts

What is Human Rights (HR)?

A complete definition, the characteristics that distinguish human rights, and how they differ from legal or moral rights.

High Yield

This is one of the most frequently asked questions on the paper — both as a short definition and as the opening line of a long essay. Examiners reward answers that combine a formal definition with the core characteristics of human rights.

Standard definition Human rights are the basic rights and freedoms that belong to every person in the world, from birth until death, simply by virtue of being human — regardless of nationality, sex, ethnicity, religion, language, or any other status.

Core Idea

Human rights rest on the idea of inherent human dignity. They are not granted by any government, ruler, or institution — they exist independently of the state and cannot legitimately be taken away, although they can be violated. The role of the state is to recognise, protect, and fulfil these rights, not to create them.

Characteristics of Human Rights

  • Inherent: They belong to a person simply because they are human, not because of any law or government grant.
  • Universal: They apply equally to everyone, everywhere, regardless of where one lives or one's status.
  • Inalienable: They cannot be taken away, sold, or given up — even if a person breaks the law, they still retain certain basic rights.
  • Indivisible and interdependent: Civil, political, economic, social, and cultural rights are all equally important; none takes priority over another.
  • Equal: All rights apply equally to all people without discrimination.
  • Non-negotiable: Governments may regulate how rights are exercised, but they cannot legitimately eliminate them.

Categories of Human Rights

CategoryExamples
Civil rightsRight to life, liberty, fair trial, freedom from torture
Political rightsRight to vote, freedom of assembly, freedom of expression
Economic rightsRight to work, right to fair wages, right to property
Social rightsRight to education, health, housing, social security
Cultural rightsRight to participate in cultural life, language rights

Human Rights vs. Other Types of Rights

It is useful to distinguish human rights from other categories often confused with them:

  • Legal rights are granted by a specific country's laws and can vary or be withdrawn by that country's legislature — human rights exist independently of any one country's legal system.
  • Moral rights are based on ethical reasoning or philosophy and may not be enforceable — human rights, once codified in international law, carry binding or quasi-binding force.
  • Fundamental rights (as used in constitutions, e.g. Pakistan's Constitution Articles 8–28) are the domestic legal expression of human rights within a particular country.
Exam tip If asked to "define and explain the nature of human rights," always include: (1) a one-line definition, (2) at least 4 characteristics, (3) one example of a category of rights. This three-part structure consistently scores full marks.
