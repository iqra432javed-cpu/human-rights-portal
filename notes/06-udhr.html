Chapter 06 · International Law

Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)

A full article-by-article thematic breakdown of the UDHR's 30 articles and its relationship to binding covenants.

High Yield

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) deserves its own focused chapter beyond the general "UN Charter" overview, because examiners frequently ask detailed questions about its content, structure, and specific articles.

Key facts to memorise Adopted: 10 December 1948, Paris  |  By: UN General Assembly, Resolution 217A (III)  |  Articles: 30  |  Drafting committee chaired by: Eleanor Roosevelt

Full Thematic Breakdown of the UDHR

Foundational Articles (1–2)

  • Article 1: All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights.
  • Article 2: Everyone is entitled to all rights without distinction of any kind.

Right to Life and Personal Security (Articles 3–11)

  • Right to life, liberty, security (Art. 3)
  • Freedom from slavery (Art. 4)
  • Freedom from torture (Art. 5)
  • Right to legal recognition (Art. 6)
  • Equality before the law (Art. 7)
  • Right to effective remedy (Art. 8)
  • Freedom from arbitrary arrest (Art. 9)
  • Right to fair public trial (Art. 10)
  • Presumption of innocence (Art. 11)

Personal and Civil Liberties (Articles 12–17)

  • Right to privacy (Art. 12)
  • Freedom of movement (Art. 13)
  • Right to seek asylum (Art. 14)
  • Right to nationality (Art. 15)
  • Right to marry and found a family (Art. 16)
  • Right to own property (Art. 17)

Political and Public Freedoms (Articles 18–21)

  • Freedom of thought, conscience, religion (Art. 18)
  • Freedom of opinion and expression (Art. 19)
  • Freedom of peaceful assembly and association (Art. 20)
  • Right to participate in government (Art. 21)

Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (Articles 22–27)

  • Right to social security (Art. 22)
  • Right to work and fair wages (Art. 23)
  • Right to rest and leisure (Art. 24)
  • Right to adequate standard of living (Art. 25)
  • Right to education (Art. 26)
  • Right to participate in cultural life (Art. 27)

Implementation and Limits (Articles 28–30)

  • Right to a social order that makes these rights realisable (Art. 28)
  • Duties to the community; limits on rights to protect others' rights (Art. 29)
  • No interpretation of the Declaration may be used to destroy these rights (Art. 30)

From Declaration to Binding Law

Because the UDHR itself is non-binding, the international community split its content into two binding covenants in 1966 to give it legal force:

CovenantFocus
ICCPR (International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights)Covers Articles 3–21 type rights — life, liberty, expression, fair trial
ICESCR (International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights)Covers Articles 22–27 type rights — work, education, health, standard of living

Together with the UDHR, these three documents form the International Bill of Human Rights.

Exam tip If a question asks you to "discuss the structure and content of the UDHR," organise your answer using the five thematic blocks above (foundational, life/security, civil liberties, political, economic/social/cultural) rather than listing 30 articles individually — examiners reward organised synthesis over rote listing.
