Chapter 08 · Debate

Are Human Rights Universal?

The cultural relativism debate — arguments for and against universal application of human rights across cultures.

High Yield

This chapter directly addresses the course outline's "Are HR Universal? (debate/discussion)" topic — one of the richest essay topics on the paper, because it requires presenting both sides of a genuine, ongoing scholarly and political debate.

The Core Question Are human rights genuinely universal — applicable to all people, in all cultures, at all times — or are they a culturally specific product of Western liberal philosophy being applied (some argue imposed) globally?

Arguments Supporting Universality

  • Shared human nature: Regardless of culture, all humans share basic needs — protection from torture, the right to food, freedom from arbitrary killing — that transcend cultural boundaries.
  • Cross-cultural roots: Concepts resembling human rights appear independently across multiple traditions and eras — the Farewell Sermon's emphasis on equality, the Magna Carta's limits on rulers, and Confucian and Hindu ethical traditions all reflect similar underlying values.
  • Global consensus instruments: Nearly every country in the world has ratified at least one core human rights treaty, and the UDHR was adopted with no dissenting votes in 1948 — suggesting broad (if imperfect) global buy-in.
  • Moral floor, not cultural ceiling: Universality does not require erasing cultural diversity — it only sets a minimum floor of protection (e.g. no torture, no slavery) within which diverse cultural practices can still exist.

Arguments for Cultural Relativism

  • Western origin critique: Critics argue the UDHR's individualist framing of rights reflects specifically Western Enlightenment philosophy, and may not match cultures that prioritise community, family, or religious obligation over individual entitlement.
  • The Bangkok Declaration (1993): Several Asian states affirmed indivisibility and universality in principle, but simultaneously stressed sovereignty, non-interference, and the importance of economic and developmental rights — signalling discomfort with how universality is sometimes applied.
  • Selective enforcement concern: Critics note that powerful states sometimes invoke "universal" human rights selectively against weaker states, while overlooking similar violations among allies — undermining the claim of neutral universality.
  • Cultural practices in tension with specific articles: Certain family, marriage, or religious practices in some societies are in tension with specific UDHR articles (e.g. Article 16 on marriage), raising the question of whose framework should take precedence.

The Middle Ground: Indivisibility-Interdependency Distinction

Some scholars argue this debate is partly resolved by distinguishing between indivisibility (no formal hierarchy among rights) and the practical sequencing of rights protection. A country may prioritise certain rights first due to limited resources without rejecting the others outright — this differs from selectively denying some rights as a matter of principle. This allows for context-sensitive implementation while still affirming the underlying universal value of all rights.

Pakistan / South Asian Angle

This debate has direct relevance to the course's stated rationale of promoting religious tolerance and cross-cultural understanding. The position generally taken in this course is a reconciliatory one: human rights principles such as equality, justice, and dignity are not foreign impositions, but find expression within local religious and cultural traditions too (as shown in the Farewell Sermon chapter) — meaning universality and cultural specificity are not necessarily in conflict if approached with nuance.

Exam tip For a "debate/discussion" style question, never argue only one side. Structure: (1) define the debate, (2) present universality arguments, (3) present cultural relativism arguments, (4) offer a reasoned conclusion or middle-ground position. This four-part structure is exactly what examiners look for in a balanced 10-mark answer.
