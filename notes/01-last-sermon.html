Chapter 01 · Religious Foundations

The Last Address of the Holy Prophet (PBUH)

How the Farewell Sermon articulates equality, dignity, and justice — and how it compares to the modern UDHR.

High Yield

The Farewell Sermon (Khutbatul Wada) was delivered by the Holy Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) on the 9th of Dhul Hijjah, 10 A.H., at Mount Arafat during his only Hajj. It is widely studied as one of history's earliest and most comprehensive human rights charters — delivered over 1,300 years before the UN's Universal Declaration of Human Rights.

Why this sermon matters for HR studies It addresses sanctity of life and property, equality of all human beings, rights of women, prohibition of usury (riba), and collective responsibility — themes that later reappear, almost article for article, in modern international human rights instruments.

Key Human Rights Themes in the Sermon

1. Sanctity of Life and Property

The Prophet (PBUH) declared the life, property, and honour of every Muslim sacred, comparing it to the sanctity of that very day and place. Taking a life unlawfully was condemned in the strongest terms — paralleling Article 3 of the UDHR ("right to life, liberty and security of person").

2. Equality of Humankind

The sermon states that all people descend from one father (Adam) and one mother (Eve), and that no Arab has superiority over a non-Arab, nor a non-Arab over an Arab, except through piety and good conduct. This directly anticipates the modern principle of non-discrimination found in Article 1 and Article 2 of the UDHR.

3. Rights of Women

The Prophet (PBUH) reminded the audience that men have rights over women, but women equally have rights over men, and instructed believers to treat women with kindness and fairness. This is frequently cited as an early articulation of relational rights and obligations between spouses, well ahead of its time for 7th century Arabia.

4. Prohibition of Usury (Riba)

All forms of interest-based dealings from the pre-Islamic era were declared void. This reflects an early economic-justice principle, connected to the modern idea of the right to a fair standard of living and protection from exploitation.

5. Collective Responsibility and Justice

The sermon repeatedly uses the phrase "Do not wrong anyone" as its central theme, and urges the audience to convey this message to those not present — establishing a continuing duty across generations, similar to how human rights instruments are meant to bind future signatories.

6. Reference to Divine Guidance

The Prophet (PBUH) left the Quran and his Sunnah as permanent sources of guidance, paralleling how modern human rights frameworks rely on permanent, codified legal texts (the UDHR, covenants, conventions) rather than informal custom alone.

Exam tip Examiners frequently ask you to compare the Farewell Sermon with the UDHR. Structure your answer using the table above: name the theme, quote (paraphrase) the sermon's position, then state the matching UDHR article number.

Significance for the Course

This chapter is placed first in the outline because it grounds the course in religious tolerance and cross-cultural understanding — themes stated directly in the course rationale. It demonstrates that the impulse to protect human dignity is not a purely Western or 20th-century invention, but has deep roots across civilizations and belief systems, which directly supports the development of cross-cultural understanding the course aims to build.
