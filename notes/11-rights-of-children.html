Rights of Children & Child Labour Debate

The CRC framework, root causes of child labour, the for/against debate, and Pakistan's legal response.

High Yield

The course outline specifically flags "Rights of Children (debate/discussion on child labour, etc.)" — making this one of the most exam-relevant chapters, especially as a debate-style long question.

Definition Children's rights are the human rights specifically recognised for individuals under the age of 18, reflecting their distinct needs for protection, development, and participation, while still being treated as individual rights-holders in their own right rather than mere dependents of adults.

The Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC), 1989

The CRC is the most widely ratified human rights treaty in the world. It sets out the civil, political, economic, social, health, and cultural rights of children and affirms that childhood is a distinct, protected stage of life lasting until age 18, during which children must be allowed to grow, learn, play, and develop.

Core principles of the CRC include:

  • Best interests of the child as a primary consideration in all decisions affecting them.
  • Right to survival and development — including healthcare, nutrition, and standard of living.
  • Right to protection — from abuse, neglect, exploitation, and harmful labour.
  • Right to participation — children's views should be heard and considered in matters affecting them.

Child Labour: Definition and Scale

Definition Child labour is work that deprives children of their childhood, their potential, and their dignity, and that is harmful to their physical and mental development — typically involving children below the legally defined minimum working age, or work that is hazardous regardless of age.

Relevant ILO instruments set the framework: the Minimum Age Convention (No. 138, 1973) sets the general minimum employment age at 15 (or 14 in some developing countries), while the Worst Forms of Child Labour Convention (No. 182, 1999) prohibits the most severe forms — slavery, trafficking, forced labour, sexual exploitation, use in armed conflict, and hazardous work.

Root Causes of Child Labour (Debate Focus)

CauseExplanation
Household povertyFamilies depend on children's earnings to meet basic needs, making child labour an economic necessity in their view rather than a choice
Lack of access to educationUnderfunded schools, distance, and school fees push children toward work instead of classrooms
Weak law enforcementLabour inspectorates are often under-resourced, allowing violations to continue despite existing legislation
Social and cultural acceptanceIn some communities, children's work is viewed as a normal contribution to family survival rather than a rights violation
Population pressureRapid population growth increases the number of dependents per household income

The Debate: Should Child Labour Ever Be Tolerated?

Arguments against any tolerance: Child labour deprives children of education and development, perpetuates cycles of poverty across generations, and violates the CRC's core protections regardless of economic circumstance.

Arguments for a more nuanced view: Some scholars distinguish between hazardous, exploitative child labour (which should be eliminated entirely) and light, non-hazardous work that does not interfere with schooling (such as helping in a family business after school hours) — arguing that blanket bans without addressing underlying poverty can sometimes push families toward even less regulated and more dangerous informal work.

The broadly accepted position in international law is the "worst forms first" approach: prioritise eliminating the most hazardous and exploitative forms of child labour immediately, while pursuing the broader elimination of all child labour through poverty reduction and expanded access to education.

Pakistan's Legal and Policy Response

  • Constitutional protection under Article 11, which prohibits all forms of forced labour, including child labour.
  • The Employment of Children Act, 1991, makes it illegal for children under 14 to work in specified occupations.
  • Ratification of ILO Convention No. 138 (2006) and Convention No. 182 (2001).
  • Ratification of the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (1990).
  • Devolution of child rights responsibilities to provinces after the 18th Constitutional Amendment (2010), leading to provincial legislation such as the Sindh Child Protection Authority Act, 2011.
Exam tip For a "debate/discussion on child labour" essay, structure your answer as: (1) define child labour and cite the relevant ILO conventions, (2) explain root causes, (3) present the for/against nuance on tolerance of light work, (4) conclude with the internationally accepted "worst forms first" position, (5) optionally add Pakistan-specific legal measures for local relevance.
