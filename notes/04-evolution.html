Chapter 04 · Historical Development

Evolution of the Concept of HR

From ancient codes and the Magna Carta to the UDHR — tracing how human rights developed across centuries.

High Yield

The idea of human rights did not appear suddenly in 1948 — it developed gradually over centuries, shaped by religious teachings, philosophical movements, revolutions, and the trauma of global conflict. Tracing this evolution chronologically is a favourite long-question topic.

Timeline of Key Milestones

Period / YearDevelopment
Ancient civilizationsCodes such as the Code of Hammurabi (Babylon) and Cyrus the Great's declaration laid early groundwork for rules of justice and protection, though not framed as universal "rights."
632 CEThe Farewell Sermon of the Holy Prophet (PBUH) articulates equality, sanctity of life, and women's rights.
1215Magna Carta (England) — limits the power of the king and establishes the principle that even rulers are subject to law.
1689English Bill of Rights — strengthens parliamentary rights and limits on monarchy.
1776American Declaration of Independence — declares "all men are created equal" with unalienable rights to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.
1789French Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen — Enlightenment-era articulation of liberty, equality, and fraternity.
1864First Geneva Convention — beginnings of international humanitarian law for the protection of the wounded in war.
1919Establishment of the International Labour Organization (ILO) — early focus on workers' rights.
1939–1945World War II and the Holocaust expose the catastrophic consequences of unchecked state power and lack of international protection for human dignity.
1945United Nations is founded; its Charter commits members to promoting "respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms."
10 Dec 1948Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) adopted by the UN General Assembly.
1966ICCPR and ICESCR adopted, turning UDHR principles into binding treaty obligations.
1979CEDAW (Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women) adopted.
1989Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC) adopted.
1993Vienna Declaration and Programme of Action reaffirms universality, indivisibility, and interdependence of human rights.

Three Generations of Rights

Scholars often classify the historical evolution of rights into three "generations":

  • First generation: Civil and political rights (liberty-oriented) — products of the 17th–18th century Enlightenment and revolutions (e.g. right to vote, freedom of speech).
  • Second generation: Economic, social, and cultural rights (equality-oriented) — emerging strongly in the 19th–20th centuries with industrialization and labour movements (e.g. right to work, education, healthcare).
  • Third generation: Collective and solidarity rights — emerging post-1945, including the right to development, a healthy environment, and peace.
Exam tip When asked to "trace the evolution of human rights," use the three-generation framework as your skeleton, then plug in the chronological milestones from the table above as supporting evidence for each generation.

Why World War II Was the Turning Point

Although earlier documents existed, the systematic genocide of the Holocaust convinced the international community that domestic law alone was insufficient to protect human dignity — a state itself could become the primary violator of its own citizens' rights. This realisation is what pushed the world toward an international, rather than purely national, framework for protecting human rights, culminating in the UDHR.
