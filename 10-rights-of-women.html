Chapter 10 · Thematic Rights

Rights of Women

International legal framework, key concerns, and the religious and constitutional dimensions of women's rights.

High Yield

Women's rights are explicitly named in the course outline and frequently tested both as a standalone topic and in connection with the Farewell Sermon and South Asian issues chapters.

Definition Women's rights refer to the human rights specifically claimed for the protection of girls and women — including equal treatment in law, freedom from gender-based discrimination and violence, and equal access to education, employment, healthcare, and political participation.

International Legal Framework

InstrumentYearKey Content
UDHR — Article 21948Prohibits discrimination on the basis of sex among other grounds
UDHR — Article 161948Equal rights for men and women in marriage, during marriage, and at its dissolution
CEDAW (Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women)1979The most comprehensive treaty dedicated to women's rights; often called an international "bill of rights for women"
Beijing Declaration and Platform for Action1995Global framework for advancing gender equality across 12 critical areas of concern

Key Areas of Concern

  • Education: Gender gaps in literacy and school enrolment remain wide in parts of South Asia, often linked to poverty, distance to school, and social norms favouring boys' education.
  • Marriage and family law: Issues include child/early marriage, unequal inheritance rights, and limited control over divorce or custody decisions in some legal systems.
  • Political participation: Despite reserved seats and quota systems in several South Asian legislatures, women remain underrepresented in senior decision-making roles.
  • Violence against women: Domestic violence, harassment, and "honour"-based violence remain significantly underreported and inconsistently prosecuted in several jurisdictions.
  • Economic participation: Women's labour force participation rates remain comparatively low in much of South Asia, often linked to unpaid domestic responsibilities and restricted mobility.

Religious and Cultural Dimension: The Farewell Sermon Connection

As covered in Chapter 1, the Farewell Sermon explicitly instructed believers to treat women with kindness and recognised mutual rights between spouses — a point this course uses to argue that respect for women's rights is not foreign to local religious tradition, but consistent with it. This supports the course's broader rationale of promoting religious tolerance alongside human rights awareness.

Constitutional Protections (Pakistan Example)

Pakistan's Constitution guarantees equality of citizens (Article 25) and prohibits discrimination on the basis of sex in respect of access to public places (Article 26) and in matters of employment in the service of Pakistan (Article 27). However, as with several other South Asian states, a persistent gap exists between constitutional guarantees and their enforcement in practice.

Exam tip A strong long-question answer on women's rights should combine three layers: (1) the international legal framework (UDHR/CEDAW), (2) specific issues (education, marriage, violence, political participation), and (3) the local religious/constitutional dimension — this layered approach demonstrates the cross-cultural understanding the course rationale emphasises.
