Chapter 07 · Core Concepts

Three Key Principles in HR

Inalienability, indivisibility, and universality — the three operating principles examiners test most often.

High Yield

The three key principles — inalienability, indivisibility, and universality — describe how human rights are supposed to operate. This is one of the most consistently examined topics on the entire paper, often appearing as both a short and a long question in the same exam.

1. Inalienability

Definition Human rights are unconditional — they do not have to be bought, inherited, or earned, and they cannot be taken away, renounced, surrendered, or transferred to another person, regardless of conduct.

This means no government, institution, or individual has the right to deprive a person of their basic rights, and a person cannot legitimately be forced or convinced to give them up — even voluntarily. Even when a person breaks the law, due process rights and protection from torture still apply.

2. Indivisibility (and Interdependence)

Definition All human rights — civil, political, economic, social, and cultural — carry equal status. None is superior to or more "fundamental" than another, and the enjoyment of one right is often connected to the enjoyment of others.

For example, the right to education is closely linked to the right to health, and freedom of expression supports the right to participate in government. Denying one right typically undermines a person's ability to fully exercise others — this is the "interdependence" dimension of indivisibility.

This principle was historically contested during the Cold War, when Western states emphasised civil and political rights while the Soviet bloc emphasised economic and social rights. The 1993 Vienna Declaration formally reaffirmed that both sets of rights are equally important and indivisible.

3. Universality

Definition Human rights apply to every person, everywhere, simply because they are human — regardless of nationality, place of residence, gender, ethnicity, religion, or political belief.

This principle is captured in Article 1 of the UDHR: "All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights." Universality is also the principle most contested by critics — particularly under the banner of cultural relativism, which argues that rights frameworks reflect Western liberal values and may not fit non-Western cultural or religious contexts equally well (covered in detail in the next chapter).

Exam tip Many students confuse "indivisible" with "universal." Memorise the distinction this way: universality is about who rights apply to (everyone), while indivisibility is about the relationship between different rights (all equal, none superior).
